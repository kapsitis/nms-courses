# What to include in the cheatsheet

1. The "Axiomatic" Starting Blocks

Before proving anything, students must agree on these "Ground Truths." These are the rules of the game:

1.1. The Parallel Postulate (Simplified): If two lines are parallel, 
alternate/corresponding angles are equal. (Allows for "Angle Chasing"). Also vertical angles, or "blakusleņķi" 
(angles that lie on the same side of a straight line and share a vertex)
1.2. The sum of internal angles for a triangle and n-gon.
1.3. The definition of isosceles triangle and its properties.
     Base angles are equal $\iff$ two sides are equal. (The primary bridge between "length" and "angle").
1.4. The SAS/ASA/SSS Congruence Criteria: This is the most important "logical unit." 
    It allows a student to say: "Because these 3 facts are true, every other part of these two shapes must be identical."
1.5. The SAS, AA, SSS criteria for similar triangles.



2. The First "Metric-Synthetic" Bridge: Area

Instead of jumping to Pythagoras, start with Area. It is the most intuitive way to teach "Quantitative $\to$ Qualitative" logic.Theorem: Triangles with the same base and height have the same area.The Logic Lesson: This allows students to prove things are parallel without measuring them. If $Area(ABC) = Area(ABD)$, then $CD$ must be parallel to $AB$.The Ratio Lemma: In a triangle $ABC$, if a point $D$ lies on $BC$, then:$$\frac{Area(ABD)}{Area(ACD)} = \frac{BD}{CD}$$Why this is key: It introduces Ratios (Quantitative) as a way to describe Position (Qualitative).

3. The "Metric-Synthetic" Bridge: Pythagoras

This is the most important theorem in the course. It connects the "Qualitative" world of angles and congruence to the "Quantitative" world of lengths.

3.1. The Statement: In a right-angled triangle, $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$.
3.2. Similar conditions on when the largest angle is acute ($a^2 + b^2 > c^2$) or obtuse ($a^2 + b^2 < c^2$).


4. The "Circle Trio" (The Synthetic Powerhouse)
Circles are the best arena for teaching "If and Only If" ($P \iff Q$) logic, which is where most students make mistakes. Introduce only these three:

4.1. Angles at the Center/Circumference: $\angle$ at center is $2 \times \angle$ at circumference.
4.2. Angles in the Same Segment: (The "Bowtie" theorem).
4.3. Cyclic Quadrilaterals: Opposite angles sum to $180^\circ$.
